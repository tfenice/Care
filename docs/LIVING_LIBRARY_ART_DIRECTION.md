# Care Living Library — Art Direction

**Version 1.0**

> A user should never think: "This is a beautiful illustration."
> They should think: "I feel like this page has been waiting for me."

---

## 1. Vision

Care is not a deck of cards.

Care is a Living Library — a collection that grows over time, organized not by number but by emotional territory. The first volume contains 69 Core Cards. Future volumes will address relationships, loss, work, family, seasons, silence, and waiting. The visual language must already support this expansion without revision.

The illustration is the silent entrance to each card. Before the user reads a word, the illustration has already set a register. It has already suggested: what kind of thing this is, what terrain they are entering, what the quality of attention will need to be.

This is not decoration. This is not branding. This is not an icon. This is a silent paragraph — one that some users will spend thirty seconds simply reading, discovering each time they look that there is something they had not noticed before.

**The visual ambition:** illustrations that feel as though they were always part of the same book. A new card added in 2028 should be indistinguishable in spirit from a card illustrated in 2024. Not identical — indistinguishable in spirit.

---

## 2. Living Library Philosophy

### The library metaphor

A library grows. It expands into new territories while remaining recognizably itself. A book added to a library ten years after its founding does not make the earlier books look dated. It is simply another volume that belongs.

Care's illustration language is a library shelf, not a product release. When we add cards, we are not "updating the app." We are adding to a collection that already exists.

### What this requires

**Consistency of spirit, not consistency of form.** Every card illustration should feel as if it was made by the same person — or by different people who had read the same books, studied the same references, and agreed on the same principles. Form varies by card. Spirit does not.

**Grammar over rules.** Rules produce sameness. Grammar produces family resemblance. The four-layer structure (Subject, Environment, Construction, Memory) is grammar, not a template. Cards are recognizably siblings without being copies.

**No dead ends.** The visual language must support:
- 69 Core Cards in Volume I
- Future volumes on themes not yet determined
- New illustrators who were not present at the founding
- Cards illustrated by different hands over many years

If a design decision would prevent any of these, it should be reconsidered.

### What "living" means

A library is alive when users return to it. A book is alive when it can be reread. A card is alive when it rewards the second, third, and twentieth encounter differently than the first.

The Memory Layer (Section 6) is the mechanism for this. The illustration grammar is the container.

## 3. 69 Card Illustration Grammar

### The current state

Volume I contains 69 Core Cards. Of these, 4 currently have full Emotional Window illustrations (A-05, P-10, H-04, G-03). The remaining 65 use the CardSignature watermark system — the category mark at opacity 0.06. Both states are complete. Neither is lesser.

The watermark state is not a placeholder. It is a quieter form of the same system.

### Priority for future illustration

Not every card needs a full illustration. The decision to illustrate a card should be based on:

1. **Emotional complexity** — does the card's concept reward a silent paragraph? Some cards are simple enough that the watermark serves them better.
2. **Category balance** — each category should eventually have a consistent number of illustrated cards.
3. **User frequency** — cards drawn most often benefit most from a richer illustration.
4. **Narrative role** — some cards are "anchor" cards in their category (the first, the hardest, the most archetypal). These warrant full illustration.

### Illustration principles across all 69 cards

**The category language constrains each illustration.** An Acceptance card may not use upward-reaching forms. A Hope card may not use forms that sink toward or below the ground. A Presence card's energy must be radial — everything referring to a center. A Growth card must have a vertical axis.

See Section 4 (Category Language) in the CARD_SIGNATURE_SYSTEM.md for the full grammar.

**Individual cards vary within the grammar.** A-05 and A-07 are both Acceptance cards. A-05's circle is small and settled; A-07's circle fills the frame and bleeds to the edges. Both are unmistakably Acceptance. Neither is the same card.

**Every illustration is a unique emotional moment.** The composition should express something that cannot be said in the title. "วันนี้ก็พอแล้ว" (Today is enough) — the illustration shows a stone that has landed. Not a circle that happens to be there. A stone that has arrived and is not going to move.

### How to maintain coherence at scale

- **Read the existing four illustrations before starting any new one.** Not to copy them, but to absorb the register.
- **Use the same three palette values.** Never introduce a fourth.
- **Apply the four-layer structure** — every new card should contain all four layers.
- **Add one Memory Layer element** — before submission, confirm that one hidden detail is present and that its meaning can be described in one sentence.
- **Run the silence test** — look at the illustration alone, without the title, for thirty seconds. Does it suggest something? Does it invite? Does it remain quiet?

## 4. Editorial References

These are not mood board images. They are traditions to study — books to look at carefully before drawing.

### Japanese botanical illustration

The reference: *Honzō Zufu* (本草図譜) by Iwasaki Tsunemasa. *Nihon Shokubutsushi* by Tomitaro Makino.

What to look for:
- The precise placement of a single stem on the page
- The use of empty space as a compositional element, not absence
- The fine line that describes the joint of a leaf, the node of a branch
- The slight asymmetry of natural forms rendered without regularization

The lesson: a plant is not symmetrical. A plant leans. A plant pauses at joints. A plant sends roots in directions that were not planned. The illustration should feel like it was observed, not designed.

### Natural history field journals

The reference: Ernst Haeckel's *Kunstformen der Natur*. Darwin's field notebooks. Audubon's *Birds of America*.

What to look for:
- Construction lines that remain in the final image — the artist did not erase their geometry
- Annotation marks that are as beautiful as the subject
- The coexistence of scientific precision and emotional presence
- Notes beside the image that explain what cannot be shown

The lesson: scientific illustration does not suppress feeling. It channels feeling through precision. The measurement mark is not mechanical — it is care.

### Letterpress atlases

The reference: nineteenth-century geological surveys. Blaeu's atlas maps. Old nautical charts.

What to look for:
- The broken stroke that indicates a fault line or an uncertain coastline
- The terrain lines that show depth through density, not color
- The construction marks at the edges of measured geometry
- The slightly imperfect circle drawn by a compass on paper that absorbs the ink

The lesson: measurement is not cold. The geologist who marked this stratum was moved by it. The astronomer who measured this distance felt something. The marks remember the person who made them.

### Zen garden design diagrams

The reference: Karesansui design drawings. Plans of Ryōanji.

What to look for:
- The horizon implied by raked lines
- The rock that needs no description — its position says everything
- The composition that functions at human scale and from above simultaneously
- The emptiness that is not empty

The lesson: the still object does not need to be surrounded by activity. What surrounds it is part of the composition.

### Architecture drafting

The reference: early twentieth-century architectural drawings. Le Corbusier's sketches. Louis Kahn's section drawings.

What to look for:
- The elevation line that shows the building and the earth it sits on in the same stroke weight
- The construction circle — the radius marked, the center dotted
- The section that reveals what cannot be seen from outside
- The guide line that is part of the final drawing

The lesson: what holds a thing up is as important as the thing itself. The ground is as present as what stands on it.

## 5. Four Illustration Layers

Every illustration contains four layers. They are not literal visual layers — they are conceptual registers that together produce depth.

### Layer 1 — Primary Subject

**What this card is about.**

The first thing the eye should find. The element that answers "what is this?"

For A-05: the circle (the day, arrived at rest)
For P-10: the concentric rings (ripples from a still center)
For H-04: the seed ellipse below the ground
For G-03: the stem and the shoot at its tip

The Primary Subject has the highest opacity in the composition (0.18–0.22). It is the first thing seen, and it is the thing the user will remember.

### Layer 2 — Environment

**Ground. Air. Water. Distance. Time.**

Every subject exists in a context. The environment layer describes that context without narrating it.

For A-05: the ground line (where the day settles), the geological strata arcs (the ground has depth)
For P-10: the wind lines (air crossing the water), the undisturbed surface (stillness before disturbance)
For H-04: the ground/horizon line (the division between visible and hidden), the soil stratification
For G-03: the ground line (the boundary between above and below)

The environment layer is secondary in opacity (0.09–0.16). It is present but not foregrounded.

### Layer 3 — Construction

**Editorial marks. Guide lines. Letterpress registration. Measured geometry. Botanical notation.**

This layer makes the illustration feel like it was drawn by a careful hand that left evidence of its process.

For A-05: the plumb line (gravity made visible), the tangent marks where circle meets ground
For P-10: the crosshair at the ring origin (the geometer's mark of the center)
For H-04: the morning light angle line (not light itself, but the direction of it), the atmospheric horizon
For G-03: the growth node marks on the stem (the joints where the plant paused)

Construction marks are at the atmosphere end of the opacity range (0.06–0.12). They are noticed on second or third viewing.

**Construction marks should feel found, not placed.** They should look as if they were already there before the illustration, and the subject was drawn on top of them.

### Layer 4 — Memory

**The faintest traces. The nearly invisible. The things discovered after repeated viewing.**

See Section 6 for full description.

## 6. Memory Layer

### What it is

The Memory Layer is not an easter egg. An easter egg is a hidden joke or reference placed for the pleasure of the creator. A memory is something that was always there, that the user discovers when they are ready.

Every card illustration should contain exactly one Memory Layer element.

### Rules

**One element only.** If there are two, neither is the memory — both are just faint.

**Opacity: 0.02–0.05 maximum.** The element should be invisible on first viewing. It should become visible only when the eye has moved slowly through the whole composition and arrived at a place it hasn't reached before.

**Position: where the eye doesn't naturally go.** If the composition has a center, the memory is at the edge. If the composition has a strong left-to-right direction, the memory is at an unexpected vertical position.

**Every memory must have a meaning that can be stated in one sentence.** Before finalizing, write that sentence in the code comment. If you cannot write it, the element is not a memory — it is a decoration. Remove it.

**The meaning must be emotionally relevant to the card's concept.** It is not enough for the element to be compositionally interesting. It must add a layer of meaning to what the card is about.

### Examples from the four hero cards

**A-05 — The zenith mark** (cx=118, cy=36)
A tiny construction cross at the highest point of the circle.
*Meaning: A geometer once measured this stone's peak. The measurement remains. The day was known, even in its quietness.*
Found by: following the circle all the way to its top, where the eye rarely travels.

**P-10 — The origin drop** (cx=248, cy=74)
A tiny filled circle at the exact center of the ring system.
*Meaning: Something specific fell here and caused the rings. Presence is not the absence of disturbance. It is attention to what broke the stillness.*
Found by: following the rings inward, past the crosshair, to the point of origin.

**H-04 — The sky beyond the sky** (y=8)
A nearly invisible broken line near the very top of the frame.
*Meaning: There is always more distance than the eye can reach. Hope does not stop at the visible horizon.*
Found by: letting the eye travel all the way to the top of the sky, past the atmosphere line at y=54.

**G-03 — The distant light** (cx=48, cy=22)
A faint circle in the upper-left corner, where the shoot is pointing.
*Meaning: Growth has a direction even when we don't know what we're growing toward. Something waits.*
Found by: looking past the bud into empty space the plant has not yet reached.

### The discovery timeline

First viewing: the user sees the Primary Subject only.
Second or third viewing: the user notices the environment and construction layers.
After weeks or months: the user discovers the memory layer. It feels like something they missed before, but it was always there.

This is the feeling of rereading a favorite book.

## 7. Future Expansion Rules

### For new Volume I cards

When adding illustrations for the remaining 65 Core Cards:

1. **Read this document and the CARD_SIGNATURE_SYSTEM.md first.** Not as a refresher — as a foundational act.
2. **Start with the category language.** What does this category's visual grammar allow? What does it forbid?
3. **Find the Primary Subject before any other element.** The subject determines everything else. If you cannot name the Primary Subject in five words, you are not ready to draw.
4. **Place the environment second.** What is this subject sitting in? What is the quality of the air or ground around it?
5. **Add construction marks that feel found.** Ask: what geometry would someone have used to place this element? Where is the center that was measured? Where is the horizon that was established?
6. **Design the Memory Layer last.** After the composition is complete, find one element that belongs in a place the eye won't naturally reach. Give it a meaning. Add it at opacity 0.02–0.05. Write its meaning in the code comment.

### For new volume cards (Relationships, Loss, Work, etc.)

New volumes may introduce subjects the Core Library does not address. Before drawing any new volume card:

1. **Confirm that the subject is in a category.** Even new volumes organize into categories. What is the emotional territory? Does it belong to the existing four, or does the new volume define new ones?
2. **If extending an existing category:** use the existing category grammar. The same circle-and-ground language for Acceptance, regardless of volume.
3. **If defining a new category:** write the category's grammar first, as a document. What is the base mark? What are the two dimensions of variation? What energy direction does it have (horizontal, radial, upward, vertical)?
4. **Never mix category grammars in one illustration.** A card that "feels like Hope and Growth" is a card that belongs to one of them and is expressed ambiguously. The tension should be in the title and body, not in the visual grammar.

### Illustration pacing

The goal is not to illustrate all 69 cards quickly. The goal is to illustrate them correctly, when the time is right.

Cards that have been drawn many times by many users are candidates for full illustration. Cards that users describe as especially resonant are candidates. Cards that complete a category's illustrated set are candidates.

There is no deadline for illustration completion. The watermark state is complete.

## 8. Animation Philosophy

### The illustration already contains motion

Every illustration should be designed so that if animation is later added, it feels inevitable — as if the composition was always waiting to move.

This is achieved through composition, not through implementation. The illustration should describe a moment that implies both a before and an after.

**A-05 — The stone settles:**
The composition implies arrival. The circle has landed. If animated: the circle approaches from above, the ground line appears as the circle descends, and the plumb line traces between them as the stone comes to rest.

**P-10 — The water remembers:**
The composition implies expansion. Rings moving outward from a center. If animated: the rings expand sequentially from the origin, fading as they reach the edges of the visible frame, until the composition settles into stillness.

**H-04 — The light arrives:**
The composition implies emergence. A morning light line appears at an angle. If animated: the light line draws itself from upper-right to lower-left, then the soil stratification lines appear sequentially from top to bottom, the seed last, the tendril reaching upward.

**G-03 — The growth is revealed:**
The composition implies slow upward movement. If animated: the root system appears first (from bottom), then the ground line, then the stem extending upward, then the shoots appearing at the joints, the bud forming last.

### Rules for future animation

- **Animations should be timed in seconds, not milliseconds.** The paper settles slowly.
- **One element appears at a time.** Never two simultaneously.
- **The Memory Layer animates last, most slowly, at lowest opacity.**
- **No bounce. No spring. No overshoot.** See `--ease-settle` and `--ease-ink` in the Paper Language System.
- **Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.** When reduced motion is enabled, skip directly to final state.
- **The animation should feel like watching something that was happening anyway** — not like an entrance or a reveal, but like arriving in the middle of a process that has been going on.

### Animation keyframes (planned, not yet implemented)

These belong in `app/globals.css` when the time comes:

```css
/* A-05: Stone settling */
@keyframes care-stone-settle { ... }

/* P-10: Rings expanding */
@keyframes care-ripple-expand { ... }

/* H-04: Light arriving */
@keyframes care-light-arrive { ... }

/* G-03: Growth revealing */
@keyframes care-growth-reveal { ... }
```

## 9. Things Never To Do

### Palette violations

- **Never introduce a fourth palette color.** The three — near-black (#2C2416), care brown (#8B6F4E), sand (#C9A97A) — are complete.
- **Never use category colors in the Emotional Window.** Category identity belongs to the pill and accent system. The illustration layer exists outside category identity.
- **Never use solid fills.** Every element is stroke-only. The paper shows through everything.
- **Never use pure white (#FFFFFF) or pure black (#000000).**

### Compositional violations

- **Never use perfect symmetry.** Symmetry belongs to logos, not to things that grew or fell or settled.
- **Never center the Primary Subject in the frame unless the concept explicitly demands centrality** (as in P-cards, where radial energy requires a center).
- **Never let the illustration compete with the text.** The text is always the hero. The illustration is the atmosphere.
- **Never add elements that cannot be described with a sentence from the category language.** If you cannot explain why an element exists, remove it.

### Reference violations

- **Never reference tarot, oracle, or divination visual traditions.** Care is not a mystical product. The illustration language comes from observation and science, not from symbolism.
- **Never reference sacred geometry.** The grid, the Fibonacci spiral, the golden ratio as decorative elements — these belong to a different visual tradition.
- **Never reference fantasy illustration.** No creatures, no creatures' silhouettes, no narrative imagery.
- **Never reference UI design.** The illustration should not look like it could appear in a design system.

### Density violations

- **Never increase density for its own sake.** Every element added must be justified by meaning.
- **Never add decorative geometry.** A circle placed because it "looks good" is not part of this system.
- **Never stack layers of similar elements to create texture.** Three faint circles is not three times as evocative as one. It is noise.

### Memory Layer violations

- **Never place more than one Memory Layer element per card.**
- **Never make the Memory Layer findable on first viewing.** If a first-time user immediately notices it, the opacity is too high.
- **Never design a Memory Layer element without writing its meaning in one sentence first.**

## 10. How Future Artists Should Extend the Library

### Before starting

Read, in this order:

1. `docs/CARE_MANIFESTO.md` — the product's core philosophy
2. `docs/PAPER_LANGUAGE.md` — the material language and token system
3. `docs/CARD_SIGNATURE_SYSTEM.md` — the category grammar and 69-card overview
4. This document — the illustration layer system
5. `lib/card-illustrations.tsx` — the four existing illustrations, with comments

Study the four existing illustrations for at least twenty minutes. Look at each one for five minutes without reading the comments. Then read the comments. Notice what you missed.

### The authoring checklist

Before submitting any new illustration:

**Layer audit:**
- [ ] Primary Subject: named in five words or fewer
- [ ] Environment: what ground, air, water, distance, or time element is present?
- [ ] Construction: what measurement or editorial mark is present?
- [ ] Memory: one element, opacity ≤ 0.05, with meaning written in one sentence in the comment

**Palette audit:**
- [ ] Only three colors used (#2C2416, #8B6F4E, #C9A97A)
- [ ] No solid fills
- [ ] No pure black or pure white

**Opacity audit:**
- [ ] Primary forms: 0.18–0.22
- [ ] Secondary structure: 0.09–0.16
- [ ] Atmosphere: 0.03–0.08
- [ ] Memory: 0.02–0.05

**Category audit:**
- [ ] Does the energy direction match the category (horizontal/Acceptance, radial/Presence, upward/Hope, vertical/Growth)?
- [ ] Are there any forms that belong to a different category's grammar?

**Silence test:**
- [ ] Look at the illustration without the title for thirty seconds
- [ ] Can you describe what you feel in one or two words?
- [ ] Does it invite, without demanding?

**Animation readiness:**
- [ ] Can you describe the natural sequence in which this illustration would animate?
- [ ] Does the composition already suggest motion through form?

**Memory discovery test:**
- [ ] Show the illustration to someone unfamiliar with the card
- [ ] Ask them to look for thirty seconds
- [ ] Ask if they noticed anything very faint or barely visible
- [ ] If they immediately find the Memory Layer, the opacity is too high

### On humility

The greatest risk in illustration is the impulse to demonstrate skill. A technically brilliant composition that draws attention to itself has failed. A quiet, simple composition that makes the user feel something they cannot name has succeeded.

Ask after every element added: "Does this help the user be quiet, or does it ask the user to look at me?"

If the answer is the second, remove it.

### On patience

The Living Library is not finished. It will not be finished. It is a library — it grows when there is something worth adding, and it rests when there is not.

Do not rush. A card illustrated in five minutes is not the same as a card illustrated over five hours. The difference is usually invisible to the eye and always visible to the feeling.
