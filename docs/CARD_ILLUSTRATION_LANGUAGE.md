# Care Card Illustration Language

A design brief for the visual identity of Care cards.

This document defines how Care cards should look and feel as illustrated objects —
not what they depict, but *how they speak* through image.

---

## 1. Philosophy

A Care card illustration is not decoration. It is not a reward for reading the text.

It is the first breath before the words.

When someone draws a card, they see the image before they read anything.
The image should create a small, quiet space — a moment of attention —
that prepares the reader to receive what follows.

**A Care card image behaves like:**

- The inside page of a handwritten letter — spare, intentional, personal
- A single object on a clean desk, noticed in a particular light
- One line of a poem, without context
- The detail you photograph when you want to remember a feeling, not a place

**It is not:**

- An illustration of the card's theme (do not literalize — do not show "acceptance" as open arms)
- A decorative border or ornamental fill
- A narrative scene with multiple elements
- A character, figure, or body part
- A symbol system (not iconography, not ideograms, not brand marks)

**The test:**

If you could describe the image in more than one sentence, it is too complex.
If you could not describe it in at least three words, it has no presence.

---

## 2. Illustration Rules

### What a Care card image contains

**Exactly one of:**

- One object
- One fragment of an object
- One natural element or phenomenon
- One geometric or organic form

**And optionally:**

- One surface (the ground, a table, a water plane, an edge)
- One quality of light

Nothing else.

---

### What varies between cards

1. **Scale** — How close are we to the object?
   - Intimate: a detail, a texture, a close fragment
   - Medium: the whole object, seen clearly
   - Expansive: the object small in generous space

2. **Position** — Where does the object sit in the frame?
   - Centered: formal, settled, deliberate
   - Offset: in motion, arriving, slightly restless
   - Edge-approaching: almost out of frame, opening outward

3. **State** — Is the object at rest, mid-gesture, or in transition?
   - Resting: complete, still, arrived
   - Mid-gesture: a drop mid-fall, steam mid-rise, a path mid-curve
   - Transitional: two states visible at once (a seed cracking, a page half-turned)

Each card occupies a different position on these three axes.
Cards within the same category share color and compositional tendency —
not the same object or the same state.

---

### What a Care card image never contains

- Multiple objects that together form a scene
- Figures, faces, hands, or any human form
- Animals
- Text, numbers, or letterforms (including Thai script)
- Decorative frames, borders, or ornamental devices within the image
- Heavy shadow, dramatic contrast, or theatrical lighting
- Symbolism that requires cultural knowledge to decode
- More than one light source
- Anything that resolves into a narrative

---

### Line quality

When the image uses line (not photographic tone):

- Weight: thin and consistent, never calligraphic variation
- End treatment: cleanly terminated or softly faded — never hard-stopped with a cap that draws attention
- Curves: either purely geometric (compass-drawn) or gently organic — never both in the same image
- Negative space: treated as equally important as the mark

---

## 3. Category Illustration Systems

Each category has a color identity, a compositional tendency, and a family of objects.
Cards within a category feel like they belong together without being identical.

---

### Acceptance (การยอมรับ) — A

**Color:** Warm stone `#9B9E94` — clay, muted grey-green, the color of still water

**Compositional tendency:**
Horizontal. Grounding. The image rests; it does not reach.
The horizon line appears often — sometimes explicit, sometimes implied.
Elements settle downward. Weight becomes presence.

**Visual world:**
Objects that hold, contain, or bear weight.
Surfaces that receive.
Things that have been set down.

Objects from this world: stone, bowl, folded cloth, water surface, the hollow of a vessel,
a thread that has been tied and cut, a vessel that is cracked but whole.

**What to avoid:**
Upward movement. Open containers. Objects in flight or falling.
Anything that implies the situation is about to change.

**The mood:**
A-category images feel like something exhaled.
The image has already arrived. It is not waiting for anything.

---

### Presence (ความใจจดจ่อ) — P

**Color:** Amber `#B8892A` — the color of candlelight, of afternoon through curtains, of tea

**Compositional tendency:**
Radial or centered. The image draws the eye inward.
Close-up scale is more common here than in any other category.
Something is being noticed — right now, at this scale.

**Visual world:**
Objects that demand close attention.
Phenomena that exist only in a single moment.
Things you notice when you stop moving.

Objects from this world: steam, water circles, light crossing a surface,
the inside of a shell, a pendulum that has stopped, cloth or paper texture seen very close.

**What to avoid:**
Expansive landscapes. Multiple focal points. Anything blurry or atmospheric.
Presence images are sharp in their attention, even when the subject is soft.

**The mood:**
P-category images feel like something you just noticed.
They have the quality of *oh* — a small recognition.
The image is not complex. It is simply *here*.

---

### Hope (ความหวัง) — H

**Color:** Sage green `#5F7A65` — the color of new leaves, dawn light through trees, early morning

**Compositional tendency:**
Upward or opening. Images tend toward the upper portion of the frame, or toward an edge.
Not triumphant movement — tentative movement. A beginning, not an arrival.
The horizon appears here too, but something is emerging above it.

**Visual world:**
Objects at the threshold of change.
Things in their earliest state.
Light that is just arriving.

Objects from this world: a seed beneath soil (not yet sprouted), the edge of a path,
a lit window at night, a single footprint, a drip in mid-fall,
one stair step, the first thread of an unspooling.

**What to avoid:**
Full bloom. Bright, saturated color. Completed gestures.
Hope images are about what is *becoming*, not what *is*.
The image should feel like a held breath.

**The mood:**
H-category images feel like a quiet yes.
Not certain. Tentative. Possible.
There is space in the frame that hasn't been filled yet.

---

### Growth (การเติบโต) — G

**Color:** Deep brown `#8B6F4E` — earth, wood grain, worn leather, the color of old books

**Compositional tendency:**
Vertical or layered. Images suggest depth, accumulation, and time.
Things that have grown, or that have been used and worn.
More texture than any other category.

**Visual world:**
Objects that carry the evidence of time.
Things that have layers, rings, or marks.
Forms that have been shaped by repeated use or gradual change.

Objects from this world: tree rings in cross-section, roots below the ground,
a worn path through grass, a spring under compression, a struck-through line,
the overlap of an old and new page, tally marks, a shadow that is longer than expected.

**What to avoid:**
Clean, new, unmarked objects.
Anything that implies instantaneous change.
Growth images are slow. They have history.

**The mood:**
G-category images feel like something that took longer than expected.
There is a sense of having passed through something.
The image is not nostalgic — it is simply true about time.

---

## 4. Card-Level Uniqueness Framework

Cards within the same category share:
- Color identity
- Compositional tendency
- General object family

Cards within the same category differ by:
- The specific object chosen
- The scale of that object in frame
- The position of the object
- The state of the object (resting, mid-gesture, transitional)

### The three-axis variation system

Map each card on these axes before choosing its image:

| Axis | Option 1 | Option 2 | Option 3 |
|------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Scale** | Intimate (close fragment) | Medium (whole object) | Expansive (small in space) |
| **Position** | Centered | Offset | Edge-approaching |
| **State** | Resting | Mid-gesture | Transitional |

No two cards within a category should share the same combination.
With 10 cards and 27 possible combinations (3³), there is room without conflict.

## 6. Working with This System

### Choosing an image for a new card

1. Identify the category (A/P/H/G) and read its visual world above
2. Choose a scale (intimate / medium / expansive)
3. Choose a position (centered / offset / edge-approaching)
4. Choose a state (resting / mid-gesture / transitional)
5. Find an object from the category's world that fits that combination
6. Verify: can you describe the image in one sentence?

### Common mistakes

**Too literal.** The card "วันนี้ก็พอแล้ว" (Today is enough) does not show a full day or a completed task. It shows a thread that has been knotted and cut — small, closed, sufficient.

**Too complex.** If you add a second object to explain the first, remove both and find one object that needs no explanation.

**Wrong category energy.** A Hope image that is fully arrived (a blooming flower) has left the category. Hope images show potential, not fulfillment. If the image feels complete and satisfied, it may belong in Acceptance or Growth.

**Narrative reading.** If a viewer's first question is "what is happening?" rather than "what does this feel like?", the image has become a story. Return to one object, one state.
